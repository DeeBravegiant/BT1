Audit Report

## Title
`instantWithdrawal()` Ignores `assetsCommitted`, Allowing Instant Withdrawers to Drain Vault Assets Reserved for Queued Withdrawals - (File: contracts/LRTWithdrawalManager.sol)

## Summary

`instantWithdrawal()` checks available vault assets via `LRTUnstakingVault.getAssetsAvailableForInstantWithdrawal()`, which subtracts only the manually-set `queuedWithdrawalsBuffer` from the vault balance. It never consults `assetsCommitted[asset]`, the accounting variable that tracks assets already promised to queued withdrawers. Because `queuedWithdrawalsBuffer` defaults to zero and is not automatically synchronized with `assetsCommitted`, any rsETH holder can drain vault assets that are committed to pending queued withdrawal requests, causing `unlockQueue()` to revert and temporarily freezing queued withdrawers' funds.

## Finding Description

`initiateWithdrawal()` increments `assetsCommitted[asset]` by the expected payout amount and locks the user's rsETH in the withdrawal manager contract. [1](#0-0) 

`instantWithdrawal()` computes availability exclusively through `unstakingVault.getAssetsAvailableForInstantWithdrawal(asset)`. [2](#0-1) 

That function computes `vaultBalance - queuedWithdrawalsBuffer[asset]`, where `queuedWithdrawalsBuffer` is a mapping that defaults to zero for every asset and is only updated by an explicit operator call to `setQueuedWithdrawalsBuffer`. [3](#0-2) [4](#0-3) 

When `queuedWithdrawalsBuffer[asset] == 0` (the default), `getAssetsAvailableForInstantWithdrawal` returns the full vault balance, regardless of how much is committed via `assetsCommitted`. `instantWithdrawal()` never reads `assetsCommitted[asset]` and has no awareness of committed-but-not-yet-unlocked queued withdrawal obligations.

When `unlockQueue()` is subsequently called, `_createUnlockParams` reads `unstakingVault.balanceOf(asset)` as the available asset pool. [5](#0-4) 

If instant withdrawals have drained the vault, this returns zero and `unlockQueue()` reverts immediately. [6](#0-5) 

The root cause is a design gap: `assetsCommitted` accurately tracks the exact amount owed to queued withdrawers, but `getAssetsAvailableForInstantWithdrawal` ignores it entirely, relying instead on a manual buffer that defaults to the unsafe value of zero. The protocol has the information needed to protect queued withdrawers but does not use it.

## Impact Explanation

Queued withdrawal users who called `initiateWithdrawal()` have their rsETH locked in the withdrawal manager and are waiting for `unlockQueue()` to process their requests. If the vault is drained by instant withdrawers before `unlockQueue()` runs, the operator call reverts with `AmountMustBeGreaterThanZero`, and no queued requests can be unlocked. Users cannot recover their rsETH or receive their assets until operators manually replenish the vault. This constitutes **Medium: Temporary freezing of funds** for queued withdrawal users.

## Likelihood Explanation

`queuedWithdrawalsBuffer` defaults to zero for all assets. The operator enabling `isInstantWithdrawalEnabled` without simultaneously setting a non-zero buffer is a realistic and likely operational scenario, since the protocol does not enforce any linkage between enabling instant withdrawals and configuring the buffer. Any rsETH holder meeting `minRsEthAmountToWithdraw[asset]` can call `instantWithdrawal()` with no special role. The attacker does not need to front-run; the window between `initiateWithdrawal()` and `unlockQueue()` is at least `withdrawalDelayBlocks` (~8 days), providing ample time. [7](#0-6) 

## Recommendation

`getAssetsAvailableForInstantWithdrawal()` in `LRTUnstakingVault` should be updated to accept the `assetsCommitted` amount from the withdrawal manager and incorporate it into the availability calculation: `availableAmount = vaultBalance - max(queuedWithdrawalsBuffer[asset], assetsCommitted)`. Alternatively, `instantWithdrawal()` in `LRTWithdrawalManager` should directly enforce that `assetAmountUnlocked` does not exceed `vaultBalance - assetsCommitted[asset]` (floored at zero), removing reliance on the manually-maintained buffer for correctness.

## Proof of Concept

1. Operator enables instant withdrawals for ETH: `isInstantWithdrawalEnabled[ETH] = true`. `queuedWithdrawalsBuffer[ETH]` remains 0 (default).
2. Alice calls `initiateWithdrawal(ETH, rsETHFor10ETH, "")`. `assetsCommitted[ETH] = 10e18`. Her rsETH is locked. Operator moves 10 ETH into the vault in preparation for `unlockQueue()`.
3. Bob calls `instantWithdrawal(ETH, rsETHFor10ETH, "")`. `getAssetsAvailableForInstantWithdrawal(ETH)` returns `10e18` (full vault balance, since buffer is 0). The check at line 231 passes. The vault is drained to 0.
4. Operator calls `unlockQueue(ETH, ...)`. `_createUnlockParams` reads `unstakingVault.balanceOf(ETH) = 0`. The function reverts at `if (params.totalAvailableAssets == 0) revert AmountMustBeGreaterThanZero()`.
5. Alice's rsETH remains locked in the withdrawal manager. She cannot complete her withdrawal until operators replenish the vault.

**Foundry test plan:** Deploy `LRTWithdrawalManager` and `LRTUnstakingVault` with a mock config. Enable instant withdrawals for ETH with `queuedWithdrawalsBuffer = 0`. Have Alice call `initiateWithdrawal` (locking rsETH, setting `assetsCommitted[ETH] = 10e18`). Fund the vault with 10 ETH. Have Bob call `instantWithdrawal` draining the vault. Assert that the subsequent `unlockQueue` call reverts with `AmountMustBeGreaterThanZero`.
