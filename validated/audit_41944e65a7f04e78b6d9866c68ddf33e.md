### Title
Relayer Deposit Permanently Redirected to Sender When Meta-Transaction Inner Receipt Fails - (File: `runtime/runtime/src/actions.rs`)

### Summary

In NEAR's meta-transaction (`DelegateAction`) implementation, the relayer pays the attached deposit for inner actions, but on failure the deposit refund is sent to the `sender_id` (Alice), not the relayer. An unprivileged user can deliberately craft a `DelegateAction` with a large attached deposit targeting a call that will fail, causing the relayer to permanently lose the deposit while the user gains it.

### Finding Description

In `apply_delegate_action`, when a `DelegateAction` is processed, the inner receipt is created with `predecessor_id: sender_id.clone()` — Alice's account ID — not the relayer's: [1](#0-0) 

The code comment immediately following this block explicitly acknowledges the consequence: [2](#0-1) 

When the inner receipt fails on the receiver's shard, `refund_unspent_gas_and_deposits` sends the deposit refund to `receipt.balance_refund_receiver()`, which resolves to the `predecessor_id` of the inner receipt — Alice — not the relayer who paid: [3](#0-2) 

The deposit refund path is defined in `Receipt::new_balance_refund`, which sends the refund to the `receiver_id` argument — the predecessor of the failed receipt: [4](#0-3) 

The architecture documentation explicitly acknowledges this broken invariant: [5](#0-4) 

### Impact Explanation

The corrupted protocol value is the **NEAR token balance**: the relayer's balance is permanently reduced by the deposit amount, and Alice's balance is permanently increased by the same amount, with no on-chain recourse for the relayer. An unprivileged user can drain arbitrary NEAR from any relayer that accepts meta-transactions with attached deposits by repeatedly submitting `DelegateAction`s with large deposits designed to fail on the receiver's shard. The relayer has no on-chain mechanism to prevent or reverse this.

### Likelihood Explanation

Any NEAR account holder can create and sign a `DelegateAction`. The attack is deterministic: if Alice controls the inner action (e.g., calls a non-existent method, or calls a contract she controls that panics), she can guarantee the inner receipt fails and the deposit refund flows to her. The attack requires only that a relayer accepts the meta-transaction — a normal operational assumption for any public relayer service. The documentation explicitly names this as a financial incentive for users to exploit.

### Recommendation

- Send deposit refunds for failed inner receipts to the relayer (`signer_id` of the outer action receipt) rather than the sender (`predecessor_id` of the inner receipt).
- Alternatively, allow relayers to specify a deposit-refund recipient at submission time.
- At minimum, enforce that relayers never attach deposits to meta-transactions unless they fully trust the sender, and surface this constraint in the protocol's validation layer rather than leaving it as an off-chain concern.

### Proof of Concept

1. Alice creates a `DelegateAction` targeting a non-existent method on any contract, with a large `FunctionCall` deposit (e.g., 100 NEAR), signs it, and sends it off-chain to a relayer.
2. The relayer wraps it in a transaction, paying 100 NEAR deposit + gas from its own balance, and submits it.
3. `apply_delegate_action` creates the inner receipt with `predecessor_id = Alice`.
4. The inner receipt executes on the receiver's shard and fails (`MethodNotFound` or similar).
5. `refund_unspent_gas_and_deposits` emits a `Receipt::new_balance_refund` to `predecessor_id` = Alice.
6. Alice's balance increases by 100 NEAR; the relayer's balance is reduced by 100 NEAR + gas, with no recovery path.
