### Title
Arbitrary `hidden_puzzle_hash` Accepted in `RCATWallet.convert_to_revocable`, Enabling Attacker-Controlled Revocation Backdoor on Victim's Wallet — (`chia/wallet/cat_wallet/r_cat_wallet.py`)

### Summary
`handle_cat` in `WalletStateManager` extracts `hidden_puzzle_hash` directly from an on-chain puzzle reveal and passes it to `RCATWallet.convert_to_revocable` without any validation against a trusted set. An attacker who holds any amount of the same CAT TAIL can craft a spend that silently converts the victim's `CATWallet` into an `RCATWallet` with an attacker-controlled revocation authority, giving the attacker the ability to spend all future coins received by that wallet.

### Finding Description

**Step 1 — Extraction from on-chain data (no validation):**

In `handle_cat`, the `hidden_puzzle_hash` is read directly from the puzzle reveal of the attacker-crafted spend: [1](#0-0) 

`match_revocation_layer` simply uncurries the first curried argument of the `REVOCATION_LAYER` mod — it is a pure structural match with no semantic check: [2](#0-1) 

**Step 2 — Passed directly to `convert_to_revocable`:** [3](#0-2) 

**Step 3 — Stored without validation:**

`convert_to_revocable` has exactly one guard — `lineage_store.is_empty()` — and then unconditionally persists the attacker-supplied hash as the wallet's revocation authority: [4](#0-3) 

There is no check against `STANDARD_BRICK_PUZZLE_HASH` or any other allowlist. That constant is only enforced in the `VerifiedCredential.is_vc()` validator, which is entirely separate: [5](#0-4) 

**Step 4 — All future receive addresses use the attacker's hash:**

After conversion, `create_more_puzzle_hashes()` is called, and every subsequent derivation uses `attacker_ph` as the revocation authority: [6](#0-5) 

The attacker can then spend any coin locked to `revocation_layer(attacker_ph, victim_inner_ph_N)` by revealing their hidden puzzle via the `hidden=True` path of `solve_revocation_layer`.

### Impact Explanation

Any future CAT coins received by the victim's wallet after the conversion are locked to `CAT(TAIL, revocation_layer(attacker_ph, victim_inner_ph_N))`. The attacker, who knows the preimage of `attacker_ph`, can spend those coins at will using the hidden puzzle path — creating conditions that redirect funds to the attacker's address. The victim's wallet UI will show the coins as received and unspent, while the attacker can drain them at any time.

### Likelihood Explanation

Preconditions are realistic:
1. The victim must have a `CATWallet` for the targeted TAIL with an empty lineage store (no current balance). This is common — wallets are created speculatively, or the victim may have spent all their CATs.
2. The attacker must hold any amount of the same TAIL (or be the TAIL issuer). For widely-distributed CATs this is trivially satisfied.
3. The victim's inner puzzle hash must be known — it is public on-chain data from any prior transaction.

The `derivation_record` check at line 1248 requires the hint to match a known puzzle hash in the victim's wallet, but inner puzzle hashes are public and reused across derivation paths. [7](#0-6) 

### Recommendation

In `handle_cat`, before calling `convert_to_revocable`, validate that `revocation_layer_match[0]` equals `STANDARD_BRICK_PUZZLE_HASH` (or a wallet-configured allowlist of trusted revocation puzzle hashes). Reject the coin silently if the hash does not match. The same check should be added inside `convert_to_revocable` as a defense-in-depth guard.
