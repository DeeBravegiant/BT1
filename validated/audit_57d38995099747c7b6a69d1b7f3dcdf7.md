Audit Report

## Title
`WrappedRSETH.burn(uint256)` Restricts Self-Burn to Privileged Burners, Breaking `ERC20Burnable` and `IBurnMintERC20` Promises - ([File: contracts/ccip/WrappedRSETH.sol])

## Summary
`WrappedRSETH` inherits from `ERC20Burnable` and implements `IBurnMintERC20`, both of which promise that any token holder can burn their own balance via `burn(uint256)`. The override applies `onlyBurner`, silently restricting this to a privileged set managed by the owner. Any wrsETH holder not in `s_burners` will have their `burn(uint256)` call revert unconditionally.

## Finding Description
`WrappedRSETH` declares inheritance from `ERC20Burnable` at line 20: [1](#0-0) 

The `burn(uint256)` override at line 115 applies `onlyBurner`: [2](#0-1) 

The `onlyBurner` modifier at lines 222–225 reverts for any caller not in `s_burners`: [3](#0-2) 

`s_burners` is an `EnumerableSet.AddressSet` populated exclusively by the owner via `grantBurnRole`. OpenZeppelin's `ERC20Burnable.burn(uint256)` carries no access control — it is explicitly designed so any holder can destroy their own tokens. `IBurnMintERC20` declares `burn(uint256 amount)` with NatSpec "Burns tokens from the sender", implying no role prerequisite: [4](#0-3) 

The override silently breaks both interface contracts. Any address that receives wrsETH (e.g., via `mint`) but is not granted the burner role cannot burn their own balance.

## Impact Explanation
**Low — Contract fails to deliver promised returns, but doesn't lose value.** Token holders cannot exercise the self-burn functionality explicitly promised by `ERC20Burnable` and implied by `IBurnMintERC20`. Tokens remain in the holder's wallet; no funds are destroyed or stolen. The broken invariant is the inability to use a publicly advertised interface function.

## Likelihood Explanation
This is deterministic and always reachable. Any address that receives wrsETH tokens but is not explicitly granted the burner role will hit `SenderNotBurner` on every `burn(uint256)` call. No special conditions, timing, or attacker sophistication is required — a simple token transfer followed by a `burn` call is sufficient to trigger it.

## Recommendation
Two options:

1. **Remove `onlyBurner` from `burn(uint256)`** so any holder can burn their own balance (matching `ERC20Burnable` semantics), while keeping `onlyBurner` on `burnFrom` and `burn(address, uint256)` which burn other accounts' tokens.
2. **Remove the `ERC20Burnable` inheritance** and update the NatSpec on `IBurnMintERC20.burn(uint256)` to explicitly document that only permissioned burners may call it, so the interface accurately reflects the implementation.

## Proof of Concept
Deploy `WrappedRSETH`, grant mint role to `minter`, mint 1 ether to `alice` (who has no burner role), then call `token.burn(1 ether)` as `alice`. The call reverts with `SenderNotBurner(alice)` and `alice`'s balance remains 1 ether — directly proving the broken invariant. The submitted Foundry test reproduces this exactly and requires no fork or external state. [5](#0-4)
