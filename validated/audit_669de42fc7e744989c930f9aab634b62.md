### Title
Balance Deposit Refund in Failed Meta-Transactions Credited to Wrong Account (Sender Instead of Relayer Who Paid) - (File: runtime/runtime/src/lib.rs)

### Summary
When a `DelegateAction` (meta-transaction) inner receipt fails execution, the attached deposit is refunded to the `predecessor_id` of the inner receipt (the `sender_id` from the `DelegateAction`, i.e., Alice), not to the relayer who actually paid the deposit. An unprivileged user can exploit this to drain a relayer's funds by crafting a meta-transaction with a large deposit attached to an inner action that is guaranteed to fail.

### Finding Description
In `apply_delegate_action` (`runtime/runtime/src/actions.rs`), the inner receipt generated from a `DelegateAction` is constructed with `predecessor_id = sender_id` (Alice): [1](#0-0) 

When this inner receipt fails on Bob's shard, `refund_unspent_gas_and_deposits` in `runtime/runtime/src/lib.rs` sends the deposit refund to `receipt.balance_refund_receiver()`: [2](#0-1) 

`balance_refund_receiver()` returns `predecessor_id` (Alice) when no `refund_to` override is set: [3](#0-2) 

However, the deposit was paid by the **relayer** (the signer of the outer transaction), not by Alice. The relayer's account was debited for the full deposit cost when the outer transaction was processed. Alice never paid the deposit, yet she receives the refund when the inner action fails.

The relayer has no mechanism to redirect this refund: the inner receipt is generated by the runtime in `apply_delegate_action`, not by a contract, so the relayer cannot call `promise_set_refund_to` on it.

The protocol documentation explicitly acknowledges this behavior: [4](#0-3) 

### Impact Explanation
A malicious user (Alice) can craft a `DelegateAction` with a large deposit (e.g., 100 NEAR) attached to an inner `FunctionCall` targeting a non-existent method or a contract that is guaranteed to revert. The relayer submits the meta-transaction and pays the 100 NEAR deposit. The inner action fails on Bob's shard. Alice receives the 100 NEAR deposit refund. The relayer loses 100 NEAR with no recourse. The corrupted protocol value is the **account balance** of both the relayer (incorrectly drained) and Alice (incorrectly credited).

### Likelihood Explanation
Any unprivileged user who can submit a signed `DelegateAction` to a relayer can trigger this. The attacker controls the inner action content and can trivially guarantee failure (e.g., call a non-existent method). The only prerequisite is that a relayer accepts and submits the meta-transaction, which is the normal relayer use case. Likelihood is **Medium**: relayers that do not pre-simulate inner actions are directly exploitable.

### Recommendation
The deposit refund for a failed inner receipt generated by `apply_delegate_action` should be directed to `action_receipt.signer_id()` (the relayer) rather than `receipt.predecessor_id()` (Alice). One approach is to set the `refund_to` field on the generated inner receipt to the relayer's account ID at construction time in `apply_delegate_action`, so that `balance_refund_receiver()` returns the relayer instead of Alice.

### Proof of Concept
1. Relayer R has 1000 NEAR. Alice has 0 NEAR.
2. Alice creates a `DelegateAction` with `sender_id = Alice`, `receiver_id = Bob`, inner action = `FunctionCall { method: "nonexistent", deposit: 500 NEAR }`.
3. Alice signs the `DelegateAction` with her key and sends it to R.
4. R submits the outer transaction (paying 500 NEAR deposit + gas).
5. The inner receipt executes on Bob's shard; the function call fails (no contract / no method).
6. `refund_unspent_gas_and_deposits` emits a `new_balance_refund` receipt to `predecessor_id = Alice` for 500 NEAR.
7. Gas refund goes to R (signer), but the 500 NEAR deposit goes to Alice.
8. Net result: R loses 500 NEAR, Alice gains 500 NEAR. Alice can repeat this indefinitely.
