### Title
Deposits for Non-Winning `propose_update` Proposals Are Permanently Locked — (File: `crates/contract/src/update.rs`)

### Summary

When `propose_update` is called, a participant must attach a NEAR deposit proportional to the size of the uploaded WASM or config blob. When any update reaches threshold and `do_update` executes, it bulk-clears **all** remaining proposals via `self.entries.clear()` — but never refunds the deposits attached to those non-winning proposals. Because `UpdateEntry` does not record the proposer's account ID, there is no recovery path: the NEAR tokens are permanently locked in the contract with no admin-withdraw, no cancel-proposal, and no refund function.

---

### Finding Description

`propose_update` in `crates/contract/src/lib.rs` accepts a deposit sized to cover on-chain storage for the uploaded payload: [1](#0-0) 

The exact amount is computed by `ProposedUpdates::required_deposit`, which scales with `bytes_used(update)`. For a full contract WASM (~1.5 MB), this is on the order of 15–40 NEAR (confirmed by sandbox tests that pass `NearToken::from_near(40)`). [2](#0-1) 

When `vote_update` reaches threshold it calls `do_update`, which removes the winning entry and then **bulk-clears every other pending entry and all votes** with no refund: [3](#0-2) 

The `UpdateEntry` struct stores only the payload and `bytes_used` — not the proposer's `AccountId`: [4](#0-3) 

Because the proposer identity is never persisted, there is no information available at clear-time to issue refunds. The contract exposes `remove_update_vote` and `remove_non_participant_update_votes` (vote-only cleanup), but no function that removes a proposal entry and returns its deposit to the proposer. [5](#0-4) 

---

### Impact Explanation

Every NEAR deposit attached to a non-winning `propose_update` call is permanently locked in the contract balance. There is no admin-withdraw, no cancel-proposal, and no expiry-based refund. This breaks the production accounting invariant that participant funds deposited for governance operations are recoverable. For a 1.5 MB contract upload, a single lost proposal costs ~15–40 NEAR per proposer. Multiple concurrent proposals (which the contract explicitly supports) multiply the loss.

**Impact class: Medium** — balance/accounting invariant broken without requiring network-level DoS or operator misconfiguration.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

Multiple concurrent proposals are a normal and tested operational pattern (the sandbox test `test_propose_update_contract_many` explicitly creates two proposals and votes only one through). Any time two or more participants independently propose updates and one wins, the others' deposits are silently lost. This is a routine governance scenario, not an edge case.

---

### Recommendation

1. Add a `proposer: AccountId` field to `UpdateEntry` so the depositor is recorded at proposal time.
2. In `do_update`, iterate over all entries being cleared and issue `Promise::new(entry.proposer).transfer(refund_amount)` for each before calling `self.entries.clear()`.
3. Alternatively, expose a `cancel_update(id: UpdateId)` function callable by the original proposer that removes the entry and refunds the deposit.

---

### Proof of Concept

1. Participant A calls `propose_update` with a 1.5 MB WASM, attaching 40 NEAR. `UpdateEntry { update: Contract([...]), bytes_used: 1_572_864 }` is stored; 40 NEAR is held in the contract.
2. Participant B calls `propose_update` with a different WASM, attaching 40 NEAR. A second entry is stored.
3. Threshold participants vote for B's proposal via `vote_update`. `do_update` is triggered.
4. Inside `do_update`: `self.entries.remove(&id_B)` removes B's entry (used for deployment). Then `self.entries.clear()` removes A's entry. No refund is issued. `self.vote_by_participant.clear()` removes all votes.
5. A's 40 NEAR remains in the contract balance permanently. There is no function in the ABI to recover it. [6](#0-5) [7](#0-6)
