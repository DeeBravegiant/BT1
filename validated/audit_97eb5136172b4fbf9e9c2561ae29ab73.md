Audit Report

## Title
JIT Deposit Front-Running Enables Proportional Theft of MEV/EL Yield from Existing rsETH Holders - (File: contracts/LRTDepositPool.sol, contracts/FeeReceiver.sol, contracts/LRTOracle.sol)

## Summary
An unprivileged attacker can atomically deposit ETH at the stale stored `rsETHPrice`, trigger `FeeReceiver.sendFunds()` to push accumulated MEV/EL rewards into the deposit pool, then call `LRTOracle.updateRSETHPrice()` to recompute the price. Because the price update distributes the reward increment across the *current* rsETH supply — which now includes the attacker's freshly minted tokens — the attacker captures a fraction of yield that should have accrued exclusively to pre-existing holders. The stolen yield is proportional to the attacker's injected share and is repeatable every reward cycle.

## Finding Description
Three permissionless entry points compose the attack surface:

**1. `LRTDepositPool.depositETH()`** mints rsETH at the stored `rsETHPrice` via `getRsETHAmountToMint()`: [1](#0-0) 

The function carries no role guard beyond `whenNotPaused`/`nonReentrant`, and the mint price is `lrtOracle.rsETHPrice()` — the last *stored* value, not a freshly computed one. [2](#0-1) 

**2. `FeeReceiver.sendFunds()`** is entirely permissionless — no role check of any kind: [3](#0-2) 

Any caller can push the full accumulated MEV/EL balance into the deposit pool at will, immediately increasing `totalETHInProtocol`.

**3. `LRTOracle.updateRSETHPrice()`** is permissionless (only `whenNotPaused`): [4](#0-3) 

Inside `_updateRsETHPrice()`, the reward is computed as `totalETHInProtocol − previousTVL` where `previousTVL = rsethSupply × rsETHPrice` uses the *current* supply (post-attacker-deposit) multiplied by the *old* price: [5](#0-4) 

Because the attacker's deposit was priced at `rsETHPrice` (old price), `previousTVL` exactly accounts for their deposit as if it were pre-existing TVL. The entire reward from `FeeReceiver` therefore appears as a surplus distributed proportionally across *all* rsETH — including the attacker's newly minted tokens.

**Why `pricePercentageLimit` is insufficient:** [6](#0-5) 

The guard only fires when `pricePercentageLimit > 0` *and* the price jump exceeds the configured threshold. For typical reward cycles (small reward relative to TVL), the per-update price increase is well below any reasonable threshold. The attacker can also scale their deposit to dilute the per-unit price increase, keeping it below the limit while still extracting yield.

## Impact Explanation
**High — Theft of unclaimed yield.**

Pre-existing rsETH holders are entitled to 100% of MEV/EL rewards accumulated since the last price update. The attack diverts a fraction of that yield to the attacker proportional to their injected share. In the PoC scenario, ~60 ETH of the 180 ETH net reward is stolen. The loss is permanent: once `rsETHPrice` is updated with the diluted supply, the pre-existing holders' entitlement is irrecoverably reduced. This matches the allowed impact "Theft of unclaimed yield" (High).

## Likelihood Explanation
**Medium.** The attack requires capital (or a flash loan for the deposit leg, though EigenLayer withdrawal delays mean a flash loan cannot cover the full round-trip). The opportunity is fully permissionless, on-chain observable (monitor `FeeReceiver` balance), and repeatable every reward cycle. The `pricePercentageLimit` guard provides no protection for normal-sized reward increments relative to TVL. No privileged access, victim mistake, or external protocol compromise is required.

## Recommendation
1. **Snapshot-based deposit pricing**: In `getRsETHAmountToMint()`, replace `lrtOracle.rsETHPrice()` with an inline call to `_getTotalEthInProtocol() / rsethSupply` so the mint price always reflects the current TVL including any pending rewards already in the deposit pool.
2. **Access-control `FeeReceiver.sendFunds()`**: Restrict the function to a trusted keeper or the `MANAGER` role so reward ingestion cannot be triggered atomically by an attacker.
3. **Deposit fee or minimum holding period**: Introduce a small entry fee or time-weighted exit mechanism to make same-block or short-horizon round-trips unprofitable.

## Proof of Concept
Single-transaction attack (all steps in one block):

```
State before attack:
  rsETHPrice (stored)     = 1.05 ETH
  rsethSupply             = 9,523.81 rsETH
  totalETHInProtocol      = 10,000 ETH
  FeeReceiver.balance     = 200 ETH (accumulated MEV rewards)

Step 1 — LRTDepositPool.depositETH{value: 5000 ETH}(0, "")
  rsETH minted = 5000 / 1.05 = 4,761.90 rsETH  (at stale price)
  rsethSupply  → 14,285.71 rsETH
  totalETHInProtocol → 15,000 ETH

Step 2 — FeeReceiver.sendFunds()
  200 ETH pushed to DepositPool
  totalETHInProtocol → 15,200 ETH

Step 3 — LRTOracle.updateRSETHPrice()
  previousTVL     = 14,285.71 × 1.05 = 15,000 ETH
  rewardAmount    = 15,200 − 15,000  = 200 ETH
  protocolFee     = 200 × 10%        = 20 ETH
  newRsETHPrice   = (15,200 − 20) / 14,285.71 ≈ 1.06258 ETH

Outcome:
  Attacker's 4,761.90 rsETH × 1.06258 ≈ 5,059.8 ETH  (paid 5,000 ETH → profit ≈ 59.8 ETH)
  Pre-existing holders receive: 9,523.81 × (1.06258 − 1.05) ≈ 120 ETH of the 180 ETH net reward
  Without attack they would have received: 180 ETH
  Stolen yield: ≈ 60 ETH
```

**Foundry fork test plan**: Deploy a fork at a block where `FeeReceiver` holds a non-trivial ETH balance. In a single `vm.prank` transaction sequence: call `depositETH` with a large ETH value, call `FeeReceiver.sendFunds()`, call `updateRSETHPrice()`. Assert that `rsETHPrice` increased and that the attacker's rsETH balance × new price exceeds their deposit amount, while computing the counterfactual price (without the attacker deposit) to quantify the yield stolen from pre-existing holders.
