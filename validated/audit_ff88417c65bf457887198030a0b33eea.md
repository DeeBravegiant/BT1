Looking at the full call chain to assess whether this is a real, reachable vulnerability:

**Deserialization path (confirmed missing validation):**

`rsa_pub_key_t::convert` reads `n` and `e` from the wire and calls `set(n, e)` with zero exponent validation: [1](#0-0) 

`set` passes `n` and `e` directly to `EVP_PKEY_fromdata` with no check that `e > 1`, `e` is odd, or `e` is coprime to `phi(n)`: [2](#0-1) 

**Encryption path:**

`encrypt_oaep` applies OAEP padding then calls `encrypt_raw` (RSA_NO_PADDING), which computes `m^e mod n`. With `e=1` this yields `m^1 mod n = m` — the ciphertext is the OAEP-padded message: [3](#0-2) 

**PVE EK blob deserialization path:**

`parse_ek_blob` only checks the version field, not the RSA exponent: [4](#0-3) 

`base_pke_ek_blob_v1_t::convert` calls `c.convert(rsa_ek)` which invokes `rsa_pub_key_t::convert` — no exponent check anywhere in the chain: [5](#0-4) 

**Why this does not constitute a reachable vulnerability:**

The attack requires the adversary to **control the `ek` blob** passed to `coinbase::api::pve::encrypt()`. In the PVE construction, `ek` is the recipient's encryption key. The party who supplies `ek` is the recipient — the same party who holds `dk` and can already decrypt any ciphertext produced under that key through the normal path. Substituting a degenerate `ek` with `e=1` gives the recipient no capability they do not already possess via `dk`.

The only scenario where this would matter is a man-in-the-middle who substitutes `ek` in transit without holding `dk`. That requires an unauthenticated transport channel, which falls under "unauthenticated transport as the only cause" — an explicitly rejected category in the scope.
